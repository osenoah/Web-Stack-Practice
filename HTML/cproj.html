A well-ordered curriculum, which produces the necessary knowledge, skills, and morals in society, often draws from philosophical sources. The philosophical dimension of curriculum design involves metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, and aesthetics.
A curriculum is considered descriptive when it projects certain values through an array of subjects in a school program. It is deemed programmatic when it outlines actions that encourage students to study specific subjects, aiming to modify their behavior and improve their outlook on life.
Curriculum design represents a theory of pedagogical causation. According to Broudy, philosophical analysis and speculative synthesis can significantly influence the formulation and justification of curriculum theory.
The speculative function of philosophy is widely accepted by analytic philosophers, unlike their empiricist counterparts who undermine the speculative nature of philosophy. Professor G. Smith defended the normative role of philosophy against Othaniel Smith, who argued, "it seems obvious that matters of fact are determined by observation. " This debate illustrates the difference of opinion that characterizes the normative role of philosophy pursued through a speculative approach. In summary, the speculative function of educational philosophy seeks to understand the nature of education, while the normative function aims to guide and improve educational practices.
